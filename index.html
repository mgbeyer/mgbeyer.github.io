Welcome to my virtual ragbag of personal information

Hello! My name is Michael G. Beyer, software engineer by profession, photographer by obsession and disorganized person by confession.
I have a broad range of interests which led to various and quite different kinds of online activities and projects over the last couple years. Widespread and also kind of chaotic and confusingly distributed projects. So I needed a place, a focal point, to provide interested parties with an overview and a stepping stone. Jobwise but also very personally, related to my hobbies and other topics I'm interested in.
So, come on in, be informed, have fun!


Professional showcase

I started my career as a self-educated web designer and backend coder in the late 90s and I have worked on many web application projects ever since. Altogether I look back at well over 16 years of work as a software developer, intermitted by periods of study. Currently I am close to completing a Master's degree in computer science from the RWTH university in Aachen, Germany (with a clear focus on research).

In addition there's always a pinch of frontend design here and there, mostly based on JQuery and CSS. Professionally I'm used to Java related frameworks and - more recently - Ruby on Rails. Personally I'm more into PHP... it just happend over time :)

ABOUT ME

Typical 80s computer kid?

I was born in 1973 in Hofgeismar, a small town in Northern Hesse, Germany, located near Kassel (famous for the "documenta" art exhibition). Since I was a kid I'm interested in computers and science. At the age of 9 I got my first personal computer, a (legendary) Commodore 64 , and soon started to learn my first computer languages (Basic, then not much later Assembler). So I guess in the end it was only natural I went the path I went and many years later started to work in the field, study in the field, even focused some of my private activities in the field.
But even though I really like all this pretty much, and in some aspects I even might be considered a "nerd" or "geek", the whole "computer thing" does not define me. It's not my obsession. To say it with the words of the famous british comedy trope "Monty Python": And now for something completely different...

L'art pour l'art

What REALLY defines me in terms of DEEP fascination or even obsession (at least according to my own personal view) is this: Art. The act of playful creation for the sake of it. I don't even care if things I do are "good", "worthy" or even "sane" in the "eyes of the beholder". What I really care for is the mere FEELING things I create can give me. So now you might have an idea what floats my goat :)

The strangely wonderful world of the 70s pop culture aesthetics

See, I'm a movie junkie right from the cradle. And in particular I'm a huge fan of sci-fi and horror movies, especially from the 1970s. I like the special aesthetic of dystopien flicks like "THX 1138" , "Logan's Run" , "Phase IV" , "Soylent Green" , "The Omega Man" or "Westworld" to name but a few. Those movies and many many more of this particular era, as well as the aesthetics, architecture, general style and play of colors of this decade altogether, had a huge impact on my personal taste in and preferences of "design" and "ambience" in general.
Oh and of course they had a huge impact on today's pop culture as well. Just look at this clip from "the Simpsons", it's outright hilarious :-) .

From movie junkie to art fan in a heartbeat

Then, when I still was a kid, I saw Ridley Scott's "Alien" for the first time and this very moment really got to me. Like a seed planted within me my fascination for the whole genre began to grow in the years to come. All this was accompanied by a strong attraction to the dark and creepy but also strangely beautiful kind of atmosphere, this movie conveyed.
And I realized there was an actual artist behind this movie, not the usual bunch of set designers. A real artist who had created the scenery which transposed this strange form of beauty to the viewer. And I'm a huge admirer of H. R. Giger in particular ever since . And art in general. So, to make it short: This cineastic experience was my "rite of pasasage" to art and the surreal.
Meanwhile the whole Alien franchise is so deeply ingrained in pop culture, it's even hard to avoid sometimes. Here's the most hilarious example I've ever seen so far, from Mel Brook's "Spaceballs" (I love this guy!)

The "professional" path I went

Yes, "professional" in quotation marks for a reason. I guess for the typical IT recruiter or HR person working for a company, the ideal structure or timeline of a (academic) resume goes something like this:

The streamlined approach to working (aka "the minion way")
Born, went to school, holds relevant degrees (bachelor, master), if there's a couple years of real field experience that's even better. Oh and the only gap in the resume is the space between characters and lines, the only variance between all those resumes lies in the names and adresses of the applicants!

My timeline is a bit different, though (aka "the curvation")
Born, went to school, completed a vocational training in a job I essentially loathed (why do it then? because "reality of life", that's why!), never really worked much in this job but worked rather this and that (had to pay bills after all), completed additional training in yet another job field I liked this time but actually never worked a single day in it (why? because the job was all about customer contact and I just didn't look presentable enough for the suits at this time), so worked this and that once again... bills, you know! .

Finally
I started to work in a job I really liked and nobody cared if I look like a hippie: My first job as a software developer. So I quit the job I currently had (which was kinda shitty anyway) and thought: "what the heck, give it a try". But I had no training qualification or degree for that whatsoever.
But it worked out a couple years. However after a job change I got really frustrated because I was still underpaid and highly underappreciated. And so I decided to go to university to get my first degree in computer science. After that I worked for many years on end in a couple more or less acceptable jobs as a web-developer (slightly less underpaid and underappreciated). But finally I got deeply bored and also saw absolutely no potential for future development whatsoever. So now in my early 40s I went back to university to try and get a master degree (computer science and philosophy).

So then what?
Well for starters I'd like to work more "research related" (for whatever that EXACTLY means). All I can tell, honestly, is: I got bored as a developer and want to learn new stuff :) I think I'm too old for a PhD now, but who knows?
So I reckon I'll go with whatever job (or further education) interests me the most. That's about all a creative person can do I guess. Let's wait and see... :)

My private path

With many job changes it comes as no surprise that often there are many changes in residence as well. Born in a small town I went to a larger city some day (to study there), after that I went to an even larger city to work there, just because at the time I thought becoming a city slicker was a great idea. Fun fact: No it was not. Ok, I do admit, urban lifestyle has its merit. It certainly has 24-7 cocktail supply if you're into it (which I totally am). But its also overcrowded, stressed out, loud and you can't breathe. So gradually I moved away from the city, the older I got, the farther. At first in Cologne I just moved from downtown to the vicinity. Then I moved to a smaller city (which was a total bummer, screw you Düren! I hate your guts!). Then I moved to a small village in the middle of nowhere. And still: Too many people too close around me. So finally I moved to a place in Belgium which provides decent space, a house in the midst of cornfields and woods, the next neighors are deer and maybe those guys . So today I seek blissful solitude. A little cabin in the woods would be fine, calm and quiet. But then there's the commute. Its daily hell on four wheels already. A nice little daily 6 hour trip from a mountain lodge to the nearest city I'd manage to find a job in just wouldn't cut it.
